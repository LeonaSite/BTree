July 4, 1946

Following the Philippines' independence from the United States on July 4, 1946, the Bureau underwent a separate re-establishment. A significant reorganization took place on October 1, 1947, through Executive Order No. 94. This order led to the merging of the Accounting Unit and the Revenue Accounts and Statistical Division, the consolidation of records in the Administrative Division's Records Section, and the centralization of all legal work in the Law Division. Simultaneously, Revenue Regulations No. V-2, dated October 23, 1947, structured the country into 31 inspection units, each headed by a Provincial Revenue Agent.

The second major reorganization unfolded on January 1, 1951, with Executive Order No. 392. This executive order established three new departments within the Bureau: Legal, Assessment, and Collection. In the same month, the Withholding Tax Unit was instituted under the Income Tax Division following the adoption of the withholding tax system through Republic Act (RA) 690.

The third significant reorganization occurred on March 1, 1954, via Revenue Memorandum Order (RMO) No. 41. This resulted in the establishment of key offices, including the Specific Tax Division, Litigation Section, Processing Section, and the Office of the City Revenue Examiner. In September 1954, a Training Unit was created.

As a step toward decentralization, the Bureau established its first two Regional Offices in Cebu and Davao on July 20, 1955, through RMO No. V-536. Each Regional Office, led by a Regional Director, was supported by chiefs for branches such as Tax Audit, Collection, Investigation, Legal, and Administrative. This marked the division of the Philippines into three revenue regions.

The organizational expansion continued in 1956, with the Bureau's Regional Offices increasing to eight and later to ten in 1957. The Accounting Machine Branch was created in each Regional Office.

In January 1957, the title of the head of the Bureau changed from Collector to Commissioner, with Jose Aranas serving as the last Collector and the first Commissioner of the BIR.

A significant development in 1958 was the establishment of the Tax Census Division and corresponding Tax Census Units for each Regional Office. This aimed to consolidate statements of assets, incomes, and liabilities into a National Tax Census.

To fortify tax payment enforcement and discourage tax evasion, the Rewards Law (RA No. 233) was enacted on June 19, 1959. This law offered informers a 25% reward equivalent to the revenue collected from tax evaders.

In 1964, the Philippines was re-divided into 15 regions and 72 inspection districts, and the Tobacco Inspection Board and Accountable Forms Committee were created under the Office of the Commissioner.

Ferdinand Marcos Admiristration

A. Ferdinand Marcos's administration in the Philippines (1965-1986), there were various tax policies implemented. Initially, his regime introduced reforms aiming to centralize tax collection and modernize tax administration. However, it was also criticized for alleged corruption, tax evasion, and a lack of transparency in tax collection processes. Marcos's era witnessed changes in tax laws, though the effectiveness and fairness of these changes remain a topic of debate among historians and economists. The tax system under the Marcos administration was generally regressive as it was heavily dependent on indirect taxes. Indirect taxes and international trade taxes accounted for about 35% of total tax revenue, while direct taxes only accounted for 25%. Government expenditure for economic services peaked during this period, focusing mainly on infrastructure development, with about 33% of the budget spent on capital outlays. In response to the higher global interest rates and to the depreciation of the peso, the government became increasingly reliant on domestic financing to finance fiscal deficit. The government also started liberalizing tariff policy during this period by enacting the initial Tariff Reform Program, which narrowed the tariff structure from a range of 100%–0% to 50%–10%, and the Import Liberalization Program, which aimed at reducing or eliminating tariffs and realigning indirect taxes.

B. Executive Order No. 206 was issued in the Philippines during the Marcos administration on January 9, 1970. This order restructured the internal revenue taxes, particularly introducing changes to the tax rates, exemptions, and other provisions. It aimed to reform the tax system to generate revenue for the government and support socioeconomic programs. Whereas, there is an imperative need for a more effective system of collecting national internal revenue taxes as a means of increasing tax collections and protecting the revenues. The President of the Philippines, utilizing his legal authority, instructs the Central Bank of the Philippines to accept payments of specific national internal revenue taxes. These include income taxes (inclusive of wage withholding taxes), estate, inheritance, and gift taxes, as well as certain percentage taxes, excluding those stemming from imports. Additionally, the directive encompasses taxes related to banks, insurance companies, franchise taxes, and amusement taxes.

C. The National Internal Revenue Code of 1977, enacted during Ferdinand Marcos's presidency in the Philippines on June 3, 1997. It aimed to consolidate and modernize tax laws. It encompassed changes in income tax, estate tax, donor's tax, VAT, and other related taxes. Key features included revised rates, expanded taxable items, and the introduction of the VAT system, aiming to streamline administration and bolster revenue collection. However, assessments varied, with critics highlighting potential loopholes and enforcement challenges. The code underwent amendments over time to address evolving economic conditions and perceived shortcomings in the tax system. It acknowledges the evolution of tax laws since 1939, the obsolescence of certain provisions, and the need for a unified and comprehensive tax code. The President of the Philippines, ordered the consolidation of all internal revenue laws into this new code to streamline tax regulations for efficient administration and guidance of taxpayers. The code's effectivity was immediate, while respecting the effective dates of prior amendments and new revenue laws consolidated into it. In addition, The National Internal Revenue Code of 1977 is a comprehensive law in the Philippines that outlines the country's tax regulations, covering various types of taxes, procedures, exemptions, and penalties. It provides guidelines for income taxes, value-added taxes, estate taxes, and other levies, shaping the structure and implementation of taxation in the country.

Fidel Ramos Administration

The year 1993 marked a pivotal moment in the history of the Bureau with the appointment of its first lady Commissioner, Liwayway Vinzons-Chato. Her tenure ushered in a period of significant transformation aimed at realizing the Bureau's vision and mission. At the heart of this transformative journey was the initiation of a comprehensive and integrated program known as the Action-Centered Transformation Program (ACTS). This ambitious program was designed to strategically realign and guide the entire organization toward the fulfillment of its overarching goals.

Commissioner Chato's visionary leadership was particularly evident in the year 1994 when the Bureau embarked on a landmark initiative—the five-year Tax Computerization Project (TCP). This ambitious undertaking sought to revolutionize the Bureau's operations through the establishment of a modern and computerized Integrated Tax System and Internal Administration System. The introduction of advanced technological solutions aimed to enhance efficiency, accuracy, and overall effectiveness in tax administration.

Building on the momentum of the Tax Computerization Project, additional measures were introduced in July 1997 to further streamline the operations of the Bureau. Executive Order No. 430 played a pivotal role in supporting the implementation of the computerized Integrated Tax System. The order brought about notable changes, including the creation of a fourth Revenue Group within the BIR. This newly established Legal and Enforcement Group, headed by a Deputy Commissioner, played a crucial role in fortifying the legal and enforcement aspects of the Bureau's functions.

Executive Order No. 430 also marked the establishment of several key services within the Bureau. The Internal Affairs Service was created to oversee internal matters and ensure compliance with regulations. Simultaneously, the Taxpayers Assistance Service was introduced to enhance support for taxpayers navigating the intricacies of the tax system. The Information Planning and Quality Service emerged as a vital component, focusing on information management and service quality improvement. Additionally, the creation of Revenue Data Centers contributed to the Bureau's capacity for data-driven decision-making and overall operational efficiency.

Executive Order No. 306, signed by President Joseph Ejercito Estrada on October 31, 2000, focused on further streamlining the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) in the Philippines to improve administrative control over certain categories of taxpayers and enhance the effectiveness of its computerized integrated tax system. Here's a summary of the key provisions:

1. Amendment to Executive Order No. 175: - The executive order amended certain provisions of Executive Order No. 175. - The goal was to reinforce the tax administration and enforcement capabilities of the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) to increase revenue collection for vital economic and developmental programs.

2. Integration of Excise Taxpayers Service with Large Taxpayers Service:

- The order integrated the functions of the Excise Taxpayers Service with that of the Large Taxpayers Service to create a well-defined institutional structure for all large taxpayers.

3. Changes in Organizational Structure:

- Paragraph 1 (1.1) of Section 1 of Executive Order No. 175 was amended to include changes in the organizational structure.

- The Large Taxpayers Service, headed by an Assistant Commissioner, was restructured to have three assistants, each responsible for Regular Large Taxpayers, Excise Taxpayers, and Administrative and Enforcement.

- The Large Taxpayers Service was composed of eight divisions, including Audit and Investigation Divisions, Field Operations Division, Assistance Divisions, Collection and Enforcement Division, Document Processing and Quality Assurance Division, and Programs Division.

- Large Taxpayers District Offices were to be established in Regional Offices with identified Large Taxpayers.

4. Abolition of the Excise Taxpayers Service:

- The Operations Group, supervised by a Deputy Commissioner, was reorganized to include the Taxpayers Assistance Service, Assessment Service, and Collection Service. - The Excise Taxpayers Service, previously part of the Operations Group, was abolished, and its functions were integrated with the Large Taxpayers Service.

5. Repealing Clauses: - All existing rules and regulations or parts thereof inconsistent with the provisions of Executive Order No. 306 were revoked.

6. Effectivity: - The executive order took effect immediately upon its issuance on October 31, 2000.

This executive order reflects the government's effort to make the BIR more efficient and responsive in its role as a revenue-collecting agency, particularly in the context of economic challenges during the Asian financial crisis.

Project R.I.P (Rest in Peace)

Project RIP was introduced by the Bureau of Internal Revenue on February 9, 2010, with the aim of strengthening the evaluation process and optimizing the collection of estate taxes. The project seeks to enhance public awareness regarding the proper payment of estate taxes. Through Project RIP, the Bureau will closely monitor the proper payment of estate taxes, an area that has been historically overlooked by the BIR. Data from the National Statistics Office indicates a significant number of deaths in previous years, with a notable disparity in the filed estate tax returns as per BIR records. BIR Commissioner Joel L. Tan-Torres anticipates a collection of approximately P10.7 billion through this initiative. The Revenue Memorandum Order No.10-2010 outlines the Bureau's strategies, including establishing partnerships with public and private institutions, engaging with decedents' families, and conducting a public awareness campaign on Project RIP. The Revenue District Office (RDO) is tasked with proactively monitoring potential estate tax cases by collaborating with various entities, such as civil registers, hospitals, and financial institutions. For tracking obituaries in newspapers, the National Investigation Division (NID) will compile a list of decedents, identifying "High Potential" cases with significant properties and potential tax payments. Coordination between the Deputy Commissioner for Information Systems Group and Operations Group will establish a Data Warehouse for potential estate tax cases, facilitating access for concerned BIR offices. The Deputy Commissioner for Legal and Enforcement Group will collaborate with the Operations Group to draft necessary issuances, policies, guidelines, and legal documents for the successful implementation of Project RIP.

Benigno Aquino III Administration

The six-year term of former Philippine President Benigno S.C. Aquino III, spanning from 2010 to 2016. The period is characterized by economic growth and allegations of elitism. The administration's emphasis on good governance is acknowledged for fostering a conducive environment for investments. However, criticisms arise regarding perceived elitism and failures in public services and infrastructure development.

Key accomplishments of the Aquino administration include the passage of the Reproductive Health Bill, the implementation of the K-12 education system, and a favorable ruling in the South China Sea territorial dispute. Despite successes, challenges and controversies emerged, such as the Pork Barrel Scam, the SAF 44 incident, and the government's response to Super Typhoon Yolanda, which faced public scrutiny.

Legal issues, including the Corona impeachment trial and the signing of the Human Rights Victims Reparation and Recognition Act, were notable events. The administration's fiscal achievements, marked by debt reduction and the Philippines' first-ever investment grade, are highlighted, as are key educational reforms like the K-12 system. The article concludes by underscoring both the achievements and challenges of the Aquino administration.

Rodirigo Duterte Administration

The Tax Reform for Acceleration and Inclusion Law (TRAIN Law), officially designated as Republic Act No. 10963, is the initial package of the Comprehensive Tax Reform Program (CTRP) signed into law by President Rodrigo Duterte on December 19, 2017.

The TRAIN Act is aimed to generate revenue to achieve the 2022 and 2040 vision of the Duterte administration, namely, to eradicate extreme poverty, to create inclusive institutions that will offer equal opportunities to all, and to achieve higher income country status. Here are some key points about the TRAIN law:

1. Income Tax Reform:

• The law lowered personal income tax rates but expanded the tax base by removing certain exemptions.

• The tax brackets were adjusted, resulting in lower income tax rates for many taxpayers.

2. Value Added Tax (VAT) Exemptions:

• Some goods and services were added to the list of VAT-exempt items, such as those related to senior citizens and persons with disabilities.

3. Excise Tax on Fuel:

• The law implemented an excise tax on fuel, which was expected to have an impact on transportation costs and, consequently, the prices of goods and services.

4. Sugar-Sweetened Beverages Tax:

• The TRAIN law imposed a tax on sugar-sweetened beverages to promote health and wellness.

5. Automobile Excise Tax:

• Excise taxes on automobiles were increased, particularly for luxury cars.

6. Social Mitigation Measures:

• The government introduced social mitigations measures to offset the potential impact of the tax reforms on the lower-income segments of the population. These included cash transfers and subsidies.

7. Infrastructure Spending:

• The additional revenue generated from the tax reforms was intended to fund the government's infrastructure projects under the "Build, Build, Build" program.
